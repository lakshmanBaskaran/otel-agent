# Group Block Management

## What groups are
Multi-room bookings tied to one event. is_block = true. Conferences, weddings, corporate offsites — these are the typical examples.

## Double-edged sword
**Good side:** volume, long lead time, predictable, mid-week fill, F&B attached, repeat potential

**Bad side:** concentration risk, lower ADR than transient, displaces higher-paying business, wash and shrinkage over time, attrition

## The real question
Not "should we take groups?" — but "is this group worth the displacement cost?"

Example: a 50-room block at €120 ADR for 3 nights = €18K guaranteed. But if you'd have filled at €180 transient, that's €27K displaced. Theoretical loss is €9K — but real loss is usually 60-70% of that, because you wouldn't have filled all of it anyway. So real net is closer to €5-6K loss, against €18K guaranteed plus attached F&B.

The decision depends on whether transient demand would have actually filled those nights at full rate.

## Three thresholds

**Block share of revenue:**
- Under 30% → transient-heavy, normal
- 30-50% → balanced
- 50-70% → group-dependent
- Over 70% → conference hotel mode, high risk

**Single-block concentration (one group's share of the month):**
- Under 15% → diversified
- 15-25% → manageable
- 25-40% → high exposure
- Over 40% → month is hostage to this group

**Group ADR vs transient ADR (displacement signal):**
- Within 10% → fair value
- 10-25% below → normal volume discount
- More than 25% below → check if it's displacing better business

## Where groups go wrong
- **Block wash** — groups don't cancel outright, they shrink. 30 rooms becomes 25, then 22. Monitor block room nights week over week, not just cancellation count.
- **Phantom bookings** — a group "confirms" 50 rooms but deposits for 25. They're hedging. Require at least a 30% deposit-to-block ratio for the booking to count as committed.
- **Late cutoffs** — group contracts include cutoff dates where unsold rooms release back to general inventory. Track these so you can backfill in time.

## Three questions before saying yes to any group
1. **What does this displace?** Check transient pace and pickup for those dates.
2. **What does this attach?** Room revenue plus F&B, AV, meeting space = total value.
3. **What's the wash risk?** Long lead time + soft deposit = high wash. Adjust rate accordingly.

A group at €120 ADR with €15K of attached F&B is worth €60K total. A group at €120 ADR with no attached spend is worth €18K. Same room rate, totally different decisions.

## Recommendations
- **Block share over 50% in a month** → confirm all blocks with deposits, lock attrition clauses, prep backfill plan
- **One group over 30% of month revenue** → call the account, confirm headcount, request earlier deposit, secure attached F&B
- **Group ADR much lower than transient** → walk away if transient demand is strong
- **Long lead time (90+ days)** → build in 10-15% expected shrinkage into the forecast
- **Recurring annual group** → renew contract with rate escalation, lock multi-year if possible

## The trap
Don't celebrate group revenue without checking displacement AND concentration.

A €20K block looks great until it cancels. It also looks great until you realize you turned away €25K in transient to take it.

But the opposite is also true: don't refuse groups just because of displacement theory. If transient pace is weak, a confirmed group is better than empty rooms.

## How this connects to other skills
- Concentration risk — single-block concentration is the dangerous combination with account concentration
- Cancellation patterns — block wash is different from transient cancellation
- OTB position — group-heavy OTB looks healthy but is more fragile
- Rate integrity — group rate decisions affect overall rate strategy
- Channel dependency — groups don't come through OTA, so group-heavy months tend to be low-OTA months
